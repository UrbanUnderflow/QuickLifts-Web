# Partnership Messaging Guide

This guide is for anyone talking about Pulse to brands, gyms and studios, and run clubs. It translates our North Star — Partnership-Led Community Growth — into concrete language you can use in decks, emails, landing pages, and in-product copy. If a sentence here could apply to any fitness app, rewrite it until it feels like something only Pulse would say.

---

## Core Narrative

Pulse is the digital home for serious fitness communities — the place where creators, gyms and studios, and run clubs host the challenge seasons, blocks, and rituals that keep their people showing up together.

Most partners already have community energy. A brand has athletes, ambassadors, and customers who care about more than a logo. A gym has members who show up for each other as much as for the programming. A run club has people who plan their week around the group run. What they don’t have is a single, shared space where those people can actually train, recover, and track their progress together over time.

Pulse is that space. We sit underneath the channels partners already use — social, email, on-site activations, group chats — and give them one challenge layer where:
- Creators can run real strength blocks, reset seasons, and race builds their followers can join with a tap.
- Gyms and studios can turn 6–8 week programming cycles and weekly rituals into shared, trackable challenges for their entire membership.
- Run clubs can turn weekly runs and race calendars into a season-long story runners can see and stick with.

Partnership-Led Community Growth means we measure success in behavior, not just reach. When a partner chooses Pulse, the win is not a one-time spike in signups; it’s a durable rhythm of join → complete → return. More people entering each season, more of them finishing, and more of them raising their hand for the next chapter — all inside a space that feels true to the culture that partner has already built.

## Messaging Pillars

Use these pillars to anchor how we talk about Pulse in any partner context. They should show up in different words depending on the audience, but the ideas stay consistent.

### 1. Creator-Led Challenges

We lead with people, not product. Pulse is built around the creators, coaches, and captains who already carry trust inside a community.

**How to say it:**
- "Pulse gives your athletes and coaches a home base for the challenge seasons they already run — with tools built for check-ins, streaks, and shared progress, not just posts."
- "Your pacers, coaches, and ambassadors stay at the center; Pulse simply gives them a better stage."

**Avoid:**
- Generic "engagement" language that makes it sound like Pulse is running the show instead of the community leaders.

### 2. Communities as the Product

For our partners, the real product is the feeling of belonging their people share — not content, not inventory, not a one-time event.

**How to say it:**
- "Pulse helps you turn ‘we show up for each other’ into a visible sequence of blocks, seasons, and rituals your community can look back on."
- "Instead of chasing new eyeballs, we deepen the experience for the people who already care most."

**Avoid:**
- Talking about community as a "channel" or "audience" to be monetized.

### 3. Co-Branded Rituals, Not Ads

Partnerships on Pulse should feel like recurring rituals your community looks forward to — not campaigns they scroll past.

**How to say it:**
- "Turn your next product drop into a four-week performance block that your ambassadors lead inside Pulse, with your brand showing up in the work, not just the logo."
- "Think Monday strength crews, Saturday long runs, and seasonal resets — co-branded experiences your people can actually finish together."

**Avoid:**
- Language that frames Pulse as an ad inventory or push channel ("placements," "impressions," "reach") without connecting back to the shared work people do.

### 4. One Challenge Layer Across Existing Channels

We don’t replace our partners’ channels; we connect them.

**How to say it:**
- "Whatever channels you already use — Instagram, email, in-gym signage, group chats — all roads lead back to the same Pulse challenge, so your community sees one clear next step instead of a pile of links."
- "Pulse sits underneath your stack as a single challenge layer that keeps the story of the season consistent everywhere."

**Avoid:**
- Overpromising that Pulse will "fix" all marketing or community problems by itself. We are the layer that makes existing efforts compound, not a magic switch.

### 5. Shared, Behavior-First KPIs

We ground every partnership in behavior metrics that matter to communities: how many people join, how many finish, how often they come back, and how many leaders are actively hosting.

**How to say it:**
- "We’ll look at join rate, completion rate, repeat-challenge rate, streak weeks, and active creators so you can see whether your community is actually moving together on Pulse."
- "Our best stories sound like: ‘We started with one six-week block and now 70% of those members have completed three or more seasons together.’"

**Avoid:**
- Leading with vanity metrics (downloads, generic MAUs) without connecting them back to shared behavior.

## Voice & Tone

Our voice comes from our values: respect for serious effort, belief in communities over quick fixes, and a bias toward specifics over slogans. The tone adapts to context, but it should always feel like a real person who understands what it means to train with others.

### Principles

1. **Plain, Human Language**  
   Write the way you’d talk to a coach, founder, or run club captain you respect. Short sentences are fine. Jargon is not.

   - Prefer: "Your members see their streaks and their friends’ check-ins in one place."  
   - Avoid: "Drive engagement with gamified social features."

2. **Specific Over Aspirational**  
   Ground claims in concrete behaviors and examples.

   - Prefer: "Turn your 6–8 week strength cycles into shared challenges your whole membership can finish together."  
   - Avoid: "Unlock your community’s full potential."

3. **Respect the Partner’s Culture**  
   We are guests in their community. We don’t tell them who they are; we show how Pulse supports what they already do well.

   - Prefer: "You already have people who plan their week around your group run — Pulse gives them a place to see the season they’re building."  
   - Avoid: "We’ll teach your members how to stay accountable."

4. **Values-Driven, Not Preachy**  
   Let the product experience and examples carry our values.

   - Prefer: "We measure success in how many people come back for the next season, not just who clicked once."  
   - Avoid: "At Pulse, we believe in authentic, values-driven growth…" (without showing what that looks like.)

5. **No Wallpaper Motivation**  
   If a line sounds like it could be on any fitness poster, delete it or rewrite it.

   - Prefer: "Help your community stack small, visible wins over six weeks instead of one big resolution in January."  
   - Avoid: "Crush your goals all year long."

### Tone by Context

- **Decks and one-pagers:** Clear, confident, and concrete. Use short paragraphs and specific examples (e.g., "Tuesday tempos and Saturday long runs," "21-day recovery resets").
- **Outbound emails:** Slightly more conversational. You can acknowledge the partner’s reality (busy teams, multiple tools) and position Pulse as a simple, specific next step.
- **In-app partner copy:** Tight and directive. Focus on what the member can do next (join, check in, finish the block) and how it connects to the community around them.

If you’re unsure whether a line fits our voice, read it out loud and ask: *Would a real coach, founder, or run club captain say this without laughing?* If not, keep editing.

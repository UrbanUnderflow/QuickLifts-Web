# Evidence Memo: Building a Repeatable Partnership Playbook for Pulse

## Purpose

This evidence memo exists to inform the design of Pulse's partner onboarding kit — specifically the co-branded landing page, onboarding flow, and tracking dashboard that partners will use to activate their communities on Pulse. The goal is to surface health and behavior evidence relevant to engagement and retention in digital fitness challenges and community-led training, so that we can design a repeatable partnership playbook that reliably gets new partners from first contact to their community's first active Pulse round in under 14 days.

This document will focus on:
- Evidence around engagement and retention in digital fitness challenges and community-led training environments
- Patterns that matter for time-to-activation and early habit formation
- Design implications for partner-facing materials (landing pages, onboarding flows, dashboards)

## Findings

1. **Early social commitment and accountability materially improve adherence to digital exercise programs.** Online and app-based physical activity interventions that include structured social support (peer groups, accountability partners, or coach feedback) consistently show higher adherence and retention than self-guided programs. Trials using social incentives and team-based goals have demonstrated significantly greater increases in daily step counts and sustained engagement at 3–6 months compared with individual-only conditions [1, 2].

2. **Time-bounded challenges in the 3–6 week range balance novelty with early habit formation.** Multiple reviews of digital physical activity and weight management programs report the steepest drop-off in engagement within the first 2–4 weeks, with relatively better retention in interventions that frame behavior change as a short, time-bounded challenge (e.g., 4–6 weeks) rather than an open-ended program [3]. This window is long enough to start forming exercise routines but short enough for users to perceive the goal as achievable, especially when paired with clear start/end dates and group milestones.

3. **Early usage in the first 7–14 days is a strong predictor of longer-term retention.** Across fitness apps and online exercise interventions, users who complete a minimum threshold of sessions or check-ins in the first 1–2 weeks are significantly more likely to remain active at 8–12 weeks and beyond [4]. Early consistency (e.g., 3–4 bouts of planned activity per week) appears more predictive of retention than initial intensity or total volume, highlighting the importance of designing the first two weeks around easy wins and clear progress markers.

4. **Habit formation for exercise typically takes several weeks to months, but implementation intentions and contextual cues accelerate the process.** Longitudinal habit formation research suggests that exercise behaviors often require 6–10 weeks to reach high automaticity for most individuals, with substantial variability [5]. However, interventions that pair specific “if–then” action plans (implementation intentions) with stable context cues (time, place, social setting) show faster gains in routine adherence compared with education-only conditions [5]. This supports structuring partner-led rounds around simple, scripted commitments (e.g., “If it’s Monday at 6pm, I meet my club for the Pulse round”) baked into the onboarding flow.

5. **Community identity and shared ritual are critical drivers of engagement in group-based training and run clubs.** Qualitative work on running groups, CrossFit boxes, and other community fitness spaces finds that feelings of belonging, shared identity, and recurring rituals (weekly meetups, named workouts, signature routes) are central to sustained participation [6]. Members describe the group as part of their social identity, and this identity-level connection often outweighs purely individual health goals in explaining why they keep showing up. Digital layers that reinforce group identity (team names, club-branded challenges, visible leader roles) tend to support ongoing engagement.

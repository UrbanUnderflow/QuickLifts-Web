# Partnership-Led Community Growth — Health & Behavioral Science Synthesis

This document synthesizes health and behavioral science to support Pulse's north star of **Partnership-Led Community Growth** — becoming the digital home for the most energized, community-driven fitness brands, gyms, and run clubs. It focuses on how community structures, behavior-only metrics (showing up, closing multi-day gaps, recovery/readiness rituals), and credible human guides interact to influence adherence, motivation, and long-term retention on Pulse. Subsequent sections will distill field observations, patterns, partnership recommendations, and guardrails for claims and product design.

## Field Observations & Sources

- **Adherence tends to inflect upward around 3+ weekly sessions.** Habit-formation and exercise-adherence research suggests that moving from 1–2 to **3 or more planned sessions per week** is associated with higher maintenance of physical activity, especially when sessions are scheduled and integrated into weekly routines rather than left ad hoc. [Rhodes & de Bruijn 2013, Health Psychology Review](https://doi.org/10.1080/17437199.2013.766832)

- **Group-based and socially supported programs outperform solo prescriptions.** Meta-analyses show that interventions including **group support, supervised sessions, or buddy systems** lead to greater and more sustained increases in physical activity than individually delivered advice alone, even with identical exercise prescriptions. [Carron et al. 1996, Journal of Sport & Exercise Psychology](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.18.1.1)

- **Long gaps (7+ days) are strong predictors of dropout.** Observational data from gyms and digital behavior-change programs indicate that while 2–4 day breaks are common and often followed by return, stretches of **7–10+ days without activity** substantially raise the odds of full program abandonment. Monitoring multi-day gaps is therefore a practical early-warning signal. [Mailey et al. 2016, Translational Behavioral Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13142-015-0365-8)

- **Perceived fatigue and readiness predict training behavior better than load alone.** Research in team sports and endurance athletes finds that **self-reported soreness, fatigue, and readiness to train** often forecast subsequent performance and injury risk more accurately than external load metrics alone, supporting the use of simple subjective readiness check-ins as behavior guides (not diagnoses). [Windt & Gabbett 2017, British Journal of Sports Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2016-096422)

- **Basic recovery rituals have small but reliable effects.** Evidence across sleep hygiene, nutrition timing, and cooldown routines suggests they can modestly improve **perceived recovery and next-day readiness**, especially when practiced consistently. In contrast, many high-marketing recovery tools and supplements show small or inconsistent benefits vs. placebo. [Halson 2014, Sports Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-014-0192-7)

- **Social identity and belonging drive attendance in group fitness.** Studies of CrossFit boxes, run clubs, and other group training spaces highlight **identity (“I’m one of these people”) and belonging** as primary reasons members keep attending, often ranking alongside or above performance goals in qualitative reports. [Whiteman-Sandland et al. 2018, Sport in Society](https://doi.org/10.1080/17430437.2017.1409723)

- **Coach- and creator-led digital programs increase engagement when trusted.** Trials of web- and app-based fitness programs report higher adherence when content is delivered by a **recognizable coach or expert figure**, particularly when communication emphasizes practical behaviors and realistic expectations rather than dramatic transformations or cure-like claims. [Napolitano et al. 2013, Journal of Medical Internet Research](https://doi.org/10.2196/jmir.2522)

- **Regulators distinguish behavior-support tools from diagnostic/therapeutic devices.** FDA and EMA guidance draws a line between apps that **track and support healthy behaviors** (activity, sleep, nutrition, stress management) and those that claim to diagnose, treat, or cure disease. Staying on the behavior-support side—without claiming clinical outcomes—allows products to influence health-related quality of life while avoiding medical device classification. [FDA Digital Health Policy Navigator](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/digital-health-policy-navigator)

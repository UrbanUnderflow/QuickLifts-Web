# Partnership-Led Community Growth — Health & Behavioral Science Synthesis

This document synthesizes health, behavioral, and community science relevant to Pulse’s north star of **Partnership-Led Community Growth**. It pulls together findings on habit formation, social support, creator influence, recovery behaviors, and regulatory boundaries to inform how Pulse designs partnerships with brands, gyms, and run clubs in a way that supports real human behavior without crossing into clinical claims.

## Field Observations & Sources

- **3+ weekly sessions as a habit threshold.** Moving from 1–2 to **3 or more planned activity sessions per week** is associated with stronger habit formation and better maintenance of physical activity, especially when sessions are scheduled and tied to stable cues. This supports designing Pulse rounds and partner programs around three anchor rituals per week rather than sporadic, high-intensity events. ([Rhodes & de Bruijn, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1080/17437199.2013.766832))

- **Community and group support amplify adherence.** Group-based support, supervised sessions, and buddy systems consistently outperform solo, advice-only approaches in sustaining physical activity. For Pulse, this reinforces that brands, gyms, and run clubs are not just acquisition channels—they become adherence engines when their existing community structures are reflected in the app. ([Carron et al., 1996](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.18.1.1))

- **Gaps of 7–10+ days predict dropout.** Longitudinal behavioral data show that **extended gaps of a week or more** are strongly associated with long-term dropout, while shorter breaks (2–4 days) are common and often followed by resumption. Pulse’s >3-day and >7-day gap detection can act as early warning systems for human outreach from partners before members silently churn. ([Mailey et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13142-015-0365-8))

- **Subjective readiness is a useful guide for behavior.** Self-reported soreness, fatigue, and readiness to train can predict performance and injury risk as well as or better than external load metrics alone. This supports Pulse’s emphasis on readiness check-ins as a behavioral compass for when to push, hold, or dial back, without making diagnostic claims. ([Windt & Gabbett, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2016-096422))

- **Simple recovery rituals matter more than flashy tools.** Reviews of recovery strategies suggest that consistent sleep hygiene, basic cooldowns, and active recovery offer small but reliable benefits to perceived recovery and sustained performance, whereas many commercial tools show modest or inconsistent effects beyond placebo. Partners who normalize these simple rituals within Pulse challenges may help users feel more ready to return, independent of access to high-end recovery tech. ([Halson, 2014](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-014-0192-7))

- **Social identity and belonging drive ongoing participation.** Qualitative research on CrossFit and other group fitness communities highlights that **identifying as "one of these people" and feeling known by the group** is a primary driver of continued attendance, often rivaling performance or physique goals. Pulse’s partnership strategy can lean into this by making rounds feel like extensions of each community’s identity, not generic programs. ([Whiteman-Sandland et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1080/17430437.2017.1409723))

- **Coach- and creator-led digital programs increase engagement.** Trials of web and app-based interventions report higher adherence when programs are fronted by a **recognizable coach or expert** who provides framing, encouragement, and realistic expectations. This underpins the importance of Creator-Led Accountability on Pulse: people often follow *someone* more faithfully than they follow an app alone. ([Napolitano et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.2196/jmir.2522))

- **Hybrid in-person + digital models support retention.** Gyms and studios that combine **live classes with digital tracking and programming** retain members better than those offering only one mode, by keeping the community connected between sessions. By helping partners mirror their live rituals inside Pulse, the platform can extend community connection across the week instead of only during sessions. ([Les Mills Global Consumer Fitness Survey, 2021](https://www.lesmills.com/us/fit-business/research-insights/))

- **Run clubs provide structure, identity, and social accountability.** Social running groups offer **structured sessions, social support, and an identity as "a runner"** that help novices and recreational runners maintain habits through motivation dips. Pulse’s run club partnerships can leverage this by making rounds the digital record and extension of those club rituals. ([Ogles & Masters, 2003](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2003-05422-004))

- **Regulators distinguish behavior-support tools from medical devices.** Digital health guidance (e.g., FDA) draws a line between apps that **track or encourage general wellness behaviors** and those that claim to diagnose, treat, or cure specific conditions. The former are often not regulated as medical devices if they avoid clinical claims, reinforcing Pulse’s behavior-only posture in all partner-facing narratives. ([FDA Digital Health Policy Navigator](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/digital-health-policy-navigator))

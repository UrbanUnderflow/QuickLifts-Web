04 - Ambient Background

The Blob System

Every screen has exactly 3 ambient blobs. They sit behind all content at z-index 0 with pointer-events none. They must never be visible as shapes - only as atmospheric glow.

Blob 1 - Purple
  • Size: 280-300pt diameter
  • Color: rgba(124,58,237, 0.20)
  • Blur: 60-70pt
  • Position: top-left, -60pt offset both axes
Blob 2 - Lime
  • Size: 200-220pt diameter
  • Color: rgba(212,234,4, 0.07)
  • Blur: 50-60pt
  • Position: top-right, +40pt off edge, ~240pt from top
Blob 3 - Green
  • Size: 160-180pt diameter
  • Color: rgba(16,185,129, 0.10)
  • Blur: 40-50pt
  • Position: bottom-left, +20pt from edge, -300pt from bottom

07 - Copy Principles

Writing for Athletes

The biggest problem in Pulse Check copy is not any single word - it is point of view. Every sentence must be written from the athlete's experience, not the system's perspective.

1
Name the feeling, not the feature
Athletes do not care about "pilot flows" and "consent steps." They care about what is about to happen to them. Lead with that. Every screen should answer: what does this mean for me, right now?
2
Short sentences win
If a sentence has more than 20 words, split it. Athletes read fast and scan. Every sentence should be able to stand alone. If cutting a word does not change the meaning, cut it.
3
"You" not "the system"
Cut words like "flow," "baseline," "pilot," and "context." Replace with what it means for the person holding the phone. "Nora gets to know you" beats "Nora gains sufficient context."
4
Set time expectations
"This takes 3 minutes" removes anxiety. "Complete your mental baseline" creates it. Tell athletes how long things take. It is the most underused trust signal in onboarding.
5
Use trust signals
"Your coaching staff set this up for you" is a trust signal. It tells the athlete this was intentional, not random. Anchor new or unfamiliar actions to a person the athlete already trusts.
6
Lead with the outcome
Do not say what the system does - say what the athlete gets. "Complete the baseline" -> "Tell Nora about yourself so she can actually help you." Same action, completely different feel.
08 - Word Bank

Words to Kill

These words appear constantly in Pulse Check copy and they all mean something specific to us - but nothing to an athlete. Every time one appears, replace it.

flow
->
steps / process / what happens next
"Flow" is a product term. Athletes know steps, not flows.
baseline
->
your starting point / first read / where you begin
Only coaches use "baseline." Athletes just want to know what they are doing.
pilot
->
program / your team / your season / the training
"Pilot" signals beta test. Athletes should feel like they are in a program, not an experiment.
context
->
know you / understand you / read you / your story
"Context" is ML language. "Nora gets to know you" is human.
operational
->
[remove entirely]
This word has never helped an athlete understand anything. Delete on sight.
onboarding
->
getting started / setup / your first steps
Onboarding is an internal product term. Athletes are "getting started," not "being onboarded."
consent steps
->
a quick yes from you / a couple of agreements
"Consent steps" sounds like a legal process. Keep it human.
data source
->
your wearable / your Oura / your Apple Watch
Name the device. Athletes know their gear. "Data source" is a backend term.
personalize
->
built for you / made for your game / fitted to you
"Personalize" is overused in SaaS and now meaningless. Show it, do not say it.
signal
->
read / score / picture / how you are doing
Only use "signal" when talking to coaches. Athletes want to know how they are doing, not what their signal says.

Pilot Invite - Full Rewrite

The pilot invite modal is the first thing a new athlete sees. This is the reference example for how every screen in the app should be rewritten.

Eyebrow Label

Before
Eyebrow
PILOT INVITATION
Feels like an internal Jira ticket. Athletes have no reference for "pilot invitation."
After
Eyebrow
Your coach invited you
Grounds the athlete immediately. They know who sent this and why it appeared on their screen.

Headline

Headline
Welcome to Founders Test Pilot
"Founders Test Pilot" is an internal program name. It means nothing to an athlete being onboarded for the first time.
Headline
You are in.
Hampton University Basketball.
Confidence first - they have been selected. Then the team name clearly. No program jargon.

Subheading

Body
"You are joining Test Basketball Team inside Hampton University Athletics. This flow will get your app setup, consent steps, and baseline lined up cleanly before the pilot begins."
Three product-jargon words in one sentence: "flow," "consent steps," "baseline." And "pilot" again. Reads like HR paperwork.
Body
Your coaching staff set this up for you. This takes about 3 minutes - then you are ready to train.
Sets a time expectation. Removes anxiety. "Your coaching staff" is a trust signal. Zero jargon.

What This Invite Means

Section
"Confirm the team and pilot look right, then continue. We will keep your current PulseCheck account and place you directly into the correct pilot flow."
"Place you directly into the correct pilot flow" is backend language. No athlete thinks in terms of being placed into flows.
Section
Here is what happens next

1. Make sure everything below looks right - your team and your role.
One clear action. "Looks right" is how an athlete thinks. No mention of accounts, flows, or pilots.

Wearables Section

Section
"Connect the wearable or health data source your staff expects as early as possible so your pilot data starts flowing cleanly.

Complete your athlete baseline promptly after joining. That gives Nora enough context to personalize what happens next inside the pilot."
"Pilot data starts flowing cleanly" and "enough context to personalize" - developer-speak. The athlete just wants to know what to do and why it matters to them personally.
Section
Two quick things to do after you join

1. Connect your wearable (Oura, Apple Watch) as soon as you can. That is how Nora tracks your recovery and readiness.

2. Complete your mental baseline. It takes 10 minutes and it is how Nora gets to know you before your first session.
Names the actual devices. Tells them WHY the baseline matters ("gets to know you"). Gives a time estimate. Every sentence is athlete-owned.

CTAs

Buttons
Not now
Join now
"Not now" leaves the athlete unsure if they will lose their spot. "Join now" is fine but generic - it could be on any app.
Buttons
I will do this later
I am ready - join team
"I will do this later" is honest - they know they can return. "I am ready - join team" is energizing and specific. The dash gives it momentum.

12 - AI Instructions

How to Design a New Screen

When any AI (Cursor, Claude, Copilot, etc.) is asked to design a Pulse Check screen, follow these steps in order. Do not skip steps.

Step 1 - Apply the page background
  • Set page/view background to #070711
  • Place 3 ambient blobs (Section 04)
  • All content at z-index 1+, blobs at z-index 0, pointer-events none
Step 2 - Build top-to-bottom structure
  • Status bar - Page title header - Scrollable content (16pt horizontal padding, 14pt card gaps) - Sticky bottom nav
Step 3 - Wrap every section in a glass card
  • Base glass card for general content - Tinted variants for pillars, metrics, shaping cells - Never opaque - No drop shadows
Step 4 - Apply typography from the scale
  • Syne bold for AI output and key metrics - DM Sans for everything the athlete reads - Never outside the type scale - Never pure white/black text - use rgba opacities
Step 5 - Apply color semantics
  • Lime = action / AI output (use sparingly) - Purple = cognitive / mental - Green = health / connection - Blue = composure / calm - Orange = streak / energy
Step 6 - Write copy athlete-first
  • Check every string against the Word Bank (Section 08) - Kill jargon before shipping - Set time expectations - Lead with outcomes, not features - Read it out loud - if it sounds like a developer wrote it, rewrite it
Step 7 - Animate only what is in Section 06
  • Avatar ring always spins - Live dot always pulses - Everything else is static - No bounces, no slides, no spring physics

What NOT to Do

x
Use white or light backgrounds anywhere
x
Add drop shadows to cards
x
Use pure black (#000000) - use #070711
x
Use Inter, Roboto, or system fonts
x
Invent colors outside Section 01
x
Make cards fully opaque
x
Omit the inner highlight gradient
x
Use purple gradient backgrounds
x
Use ALL CAPS for anything except eyebrow labels
x
Use white text on a Lime (#D4EA04) button
x
Write copy from the system's point of view
x
Use "flow," "pilot," "baseline," or "onboarding" in user-facing copy
